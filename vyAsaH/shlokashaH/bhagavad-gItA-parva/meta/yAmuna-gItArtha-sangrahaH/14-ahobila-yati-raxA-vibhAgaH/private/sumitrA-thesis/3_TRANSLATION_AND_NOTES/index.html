ŚLOKA I

It is clearly established in the Śāstra, namely Bhagavadgītā that Lord Nārāyaṇa is the Supreme Brahman,1 who is to be attained by bhakti alone.2 And, that bhakti is to be accomplished by the performance of one’s own prescribed rites,3 by the knowledge of the true nature of the self4 and by non-attachment to worldly objects.5

NOTES:

  1. According to Viśiṣṭādvaita, Lord Nārāyaṇa along with his consort Lakṣmī is the Supreme Reality. The Bhagavadgītā, which is an epitome of the Upaniṣads, mentions Him as the Supreme Reality. See also GS.V., p. 2:

    tattvaṃ jijñāsamānānāṃ hetubhiḥ sarvatomukhaiḥ /‌
    tattvam eko mahāyogī harirnārāyaṇātparaḥ //‌

    See p. 2 of the present dissertation: “ataḥ sakalopaniṣat-sāra-saṅkalanātmikāsu bhagavadgītāsvapi para-tattva-bhūta-para-brahmaiva prādhānyena pratipādyam.”

[[P69]]

  1. mahanīyaviṣaye prītiḥ bhaktiḥ. Bhakti is the sole means of attaining the Supreme, is stated thus: “bhaktaikalabhye puruṣe purāṇe…” See GS.V, p. 3.
  2. Svadharmaḥ. The word is interpreted as sve dharmaḥ (wherein the usage is in the locative case). It is also interpreted as svasya dharmaḥ (wherein the usage is with the genitive case-ending). By both interpretations, one’s own enjoined duties in accordance with one’s class and stage of life is intended. According to the commentator, the word sva at the beginning also conveys the sense of benediction. GS.V, p. 3. Cf. ubhayathāpi svavarṇāśramadharma ityarthaḥ, GS.V, p. 3.
  3. The knowledge of the self means the knowledge of one’s own self whose essential nature consists in being subordinate to the Supreme Lord: jñānam atra pāraśeṣataikarasa-svātma-viṣayaṃ jñānam, GS.V, p. 3.
  4. “Non-attachment” (Vairāgya) means a strong dislike [[P70]] towards anything other than the Supreme Lord. Cf. paramātmani yo rakto virakto’paramātmani, GS.V, p. 3; also cf. GSR, p. 4.

ŚLOKA II

In the first six chapters the performance of karma-yoga and jñāna-yoga is enjoined6 for the purpose of the experience of the self.7 This jñāna-yoga and karma-yoga, when performed with the view to please the Lord, lead to the yoga and through which the enjoyment of the self.8

NOTES:

  1. The performance of the daily and occasional rites that are enjoined according to one’s own capacity. Cf. karmayogo nāma devārcanaprabhṛtiṣu daśāsu madhye yathāvagamaṃ yathāśakti yathāruci vā … nityanaimittika-karmajātam …. nityānuṣṭhānam, GS.V, pp. 6–7.
    Jñānayoga is stated as: jñānayogo nāma uktakarmayogena labdhādhikārasya karmayogatvaveṣaṃ parityajya nityanaimittikadharmānuṣṭhānam, GS.V, p. 6.

[[P71]]

  1. ātmanaḥ jīvātmanaḥ anubhūtirūpā siddhiḥ, anubhūti-śabdena sukharūpatvam ucyate, tathā ca ātmano niratiśayasukharūpa-sākṣātkāretyarthaḥ (GS.V., p. 6).
  2. The performance of the karma to please the Lord means “the performance of those actions without any attachment to the result and resigning them to the Lord”. Cf. paramātma-prītyarthatvaṃ phalāntara-rahityādi-buddhi-viśeṣeṇa parikarmitetyarthaḥ (GS.V., p. 6).

ŚLOKA III

In the next six chapters bhakti-yoga is expounded9 in detail10 which is accomplished by the performance of the jñāna-yoga and karma-yoga for the purpose of the enjoyment of the Supreme Reality, namely, Bhagavan11 in His true nature.12

NOTES:

  1. By the first six chapters, and by the subsequent six chapters, the definition of karma, jñāna and the bhakti-yogas which are the means, and the nature of [[P72]] the three Realities, namely, sentient, insentient and the Lord are elaborately dealt with (GS.V., p. 9).
  1. “Detailed exposition” means the true nature of bhakti along with its accessories and the result achieved by such a bhakti (GS.V., p. 9).
  2. The term bhaga in the word bhagavān is primarily applicable only in the case of Lord Nārāyaṇa, that is only to the one who has the six qualities, namely, jñāna, bala, aiśvarya, śakti, vīrya and tejas, in inexhaustible measure without any imperfection. This word is figuratively applicable in the case of others (GS.V., p. 8). Also see VP., 6-5-77.
  3. His true nature means the unlimited form of the Lord as conceived by the mind (GS.V., p. 8).

ŚLOKA IV

Primordial matter,13 the nature of the individual souls,14 the evolutes of prakṛti such as mahat etc.,15 the nature of the Lord who is the controller of all these entities,16 the distinction between them,17 karma-yoga, jñāna-yoga, bhakti-yoga [[P73]] and such other issues which subserve the previous two parts are discussed in the last six chapters.

NOTES:

  1. This is the insentient matter which is the causal element for the creation of the world (GS.V., p. 10).
  2. Both the bound and the released souls are meant here (GS.V., p. 10).
  3. The evolutes of prakṛti commencing from mahat and ending with the physical bodies which are the modification of it are meant here (GS.V., p. 10).
  4. Distinction of their essential nature.
  5. The last six chapters, which subserve the previous two parts is divided into two halves. The first three deal with the three Realities (the insentient, the sentient and the Lord) and the subsequent three deal with the performance of karma-yoga etc. (GS.V., p. 10).

ŚLOKA V

The prelude to the Gītāśāstra18 was commenced under the pretext of instructing for the sake of Arjuna, the [[P74]] son of Pṛthā, whose mind was perturbed because of his attachment and mercy at an improper time,19 towards the undeserving ones20 and elders (respectively), because of the thought as to what was righteousness as unrighteousness21 and who had surrendered himself unto the Lord.22

NOTES:

  1. The Bhagavadgītā is in eighteen chapters. In the first verse, the import of the entire Bhagavadgītā was stated. By the next three verses, the summary of the three sections, each section comprising six chapters, was explained. In the next eighteen verses, beginning from the present one, the summary of each chapter is being explained. Thus in the verse, the import of the ślokas commencing from the beginning of the text and ending with tamuvāca hṛṣīkeśaḥ etc., (II.10) is summarised (GS.V., p. 12).

[[P75]]

  1. Battlefield is not the place for affection or mercy. Cf. Gītārthasaṅgraha with the Tamil Commentary of H.H. the 42nd Pontiff, p. 4.
  2. The attachment and mercy towards Duryodhana and others are meant here. GS.V, p. 14.
  3. As in the instances of the shell-silver illusion. GS.V, p. 11.
  4. Cf. śiṣyaste’haṃ śādhi māṃ tvāṃ prapannam, Bh.G. II.7. Also see GS.V, p. 11.

ŚLOKA VI

In the second chapter, the eternal nature of the individual soul,23 the performance of karma without attaching importance to its result,24 which leads to the state known as sthitadhīḥ,25 and the knowledge regarding the self and yoga26 were taught in order to remove the illusion of Arjuna.

NOTES:

  1. Vide: Bh.G.bhāṣya, p. 127 and Bh.G. verses 16–25.
  1. One who has a steadfast (mind) devotion in knowledge is called a sthitadhīḥ. GS.V, p. 13.
  2. Yoga here refers to karma-yoga. GS.V, p. 13.
  3. The illusion referred to here refers to the body-soul illusion.

[[P76]]

ŚLOKA VII

In the third chapter is elaborated the performance of the enjoined duties without attaching any importance to its result,27 with a view to protect the world,28 and resigning the agency either to the guṇas29 or to the Supreme Lord.30

NOTES:

  1. The word asaktyā in the text conveys non-attachment to results such as heavens etc. Cf. GS.V., p. 14. Also see GSR, p. 35.
  1. There would be no unwelcome result by the non-performance of duties for a jñāna-yogin, yet by performing the ordained duties he prompts others to perform their duties. This is what is meant here by the term lokarakṣāyai. Cf. GS.V, p. 15.
  2. The guṇas are sattva, rajas and tamas of prakṛti. By this, the idea of the BG text “the qualities of the insentient matter perform all action” etc. (III.27) is brought out. See GS.V., p. 14.

[[P77]]

  1. Resigning the agency to the Supreme Lord is stated by the Gītā text itself (III.30). It is because the Lord is the cause of all and is the controller of everything.

ŚLOKA VIII

In the fourth chapter is detailed the greatness of Him in His incarnations which is incidental,31 karma-yoga which is of the form of knowledge,32 its varieties33 and the importance of knowledge that is the aspect of karma-yoga.34

NOTES:

  1. The Lord’s greatness remains unaffected even though He incarnates in various forms such as matsya, kūrma, etc. Here, explaining the greatness of the Lord is incidental because the Lord instructed this karma-yoga in previous ages such as kṛta, tretā, etc., to Vivasvān, who in turn handed down this instruction traditionally to his descendants, and the same karma-yoga is being instructed by the Lord now. Arjuna has a doubt as to how Lord Kṛṣṇa Himself would have instructed [[P78]] this to those persons. In order to remove this doubt, the Lord explains the true nature of His incarnations. Cf. Bh.Gītā-bhāṣya, pp. 262–263. By this, the meaning of the Bhagavad Gītā text (IV.9) is brought out. Also cf. GS.V, p. 16.
  1. BG. IV.18.
  2. The varieties are such as idol worship, controlling the vital air, etc., and one of twelve kinds. See GS.V, p. 16; GSR, p. 39.
  3. The importance is stated in the BG text IV.33. The same idea is expressed by the statement jñānasya māhātmyam.

ŚLOKA IX

That the practice of karma-yoga is convenient which35 leads to the desired result quickly,36 the aiding factors37 in karma-yoga, and the mode of practice by which self-realisation38 is attained—all these are discussed in the fifth chapter.39

NOTES:

[[P79]]

  1. Karma-yoga is easy to practice when compared to jñāna-yoga. Further, this karma-yoga leads to the means of self-realisation quickly. By this, the idea of the BG text V.3 is brought out. Cf. GS.V, p. 16 and GSR, p. 43.
  1. The idea of the BG verse V.6 is intended by the expression śīghryam in the text.
  2. The aiding factors here mean the modus operandi in the performance of karmas. The aspirant must perform the karma with the thought that it is only the sense-organs that perform those actions and that he is not the agent. Cf. GS.V, p. 17 and GSR, p. 43.
  3. By the expression ‘prakāra’ in the text, the idea of the text BG. V.20 is conveyed.
  4. “Self-realisation” means the true nature of the individual soul. Though the individual souls reside in various bodies, they are identical in their essential nature. Thus self-realisation is the knowledge that all the individual souls are innately pure and identical. Cf. GS.V, p. 17; GS.V, p. 45.

[[P80]]

ŚLOKA X

The following five issues are explained in the sixth chapter:40 they are the instruction in regard to the41 practice of meditation,42 the four types of yogins who43 achieve self-realisation, the aiding factors44 of that self-realisation, the attainment of that yoga, and the superiority of meditation upon the Lord Himself.45

NOTES:

  1. The Gītārthasaṅgraha mentions that five topics are discussed in the sixth chapter, while the Gītābhāṣya of Rāmānuja states that only one subject, namely, Yogābhyāsavidhi, is discussed in the sixth. Vedānta Deśika, and following him Nārāyaṇamuni (the author of the present commentary), state that the subject matter mentioned in the fifth chapter of BG (V.27), [[P81]] which is Yogābhyāsavidhi, is elaborated here, and hence it is the predominant subject matter. The other four matters are related to that main topic. See GS.V, p. 18; GSR, p. 47.
  1. The practice of meditation is mentioned in the BG text VI.10 thus: “Remaining in solitude, the yogin should constantly contemplate in the heart with mind and body subdued and free from hope and possession”.
  2. The four types of self-realised (yogins) are mentioned in the four ślokas of the BG text VI.29–32, and following that, the term yogī caturdhā is used here in the text. Cf. GS.V, p. 18.
  3. The aiding factors are abhyāsa (practice) and vairāgya (absence of desire).
  4. “Attainment of yoga” here means that although one may be unable to complete the yoga (due to interrupted practice), no sin would accrue. On the contrary, there is the attainment of a meritorious world, [[P82]] birth in a noble family to continue that meditation in the next birth, etc. He will be able to complete the yoga that was commenced in some other birth. This is the summarised idea of the BG text VI.37 ff.
  5. “Superiority of meditation upon the Lord” is stated here because in the next six chapters, bhakti-yoga (which is the means of realising the Supreme) will be stated. As a prelude to that, meditation upon the Supreme Lord, which is higher than self-realisation, is stated here.

ŚLOKA XI

The true nature of His own self (that is, the Supreme Lord),46 His concealment by the insentient matter,47 whole-hearted surrender unto Him,48 distinction among the devotees of Him,49 and the superiority of those devotees who are devoted to Him (for Himself)50 are elaborated in the seventh chapter.

NOTES:

  1. The true nature of the Lord is that He is the śeṣin (the principal entity) of the sentient and insentient entities. He is the cause of everything, the supporter and controller of all, and is conveyed by all words. Cf. GS.V., p. 19.
  1. It is the qualities, namely, sattva, rajas and tamas, that conceal the true nature of the Lord. Since prakṛti is the substratum of those qualities, it is spoken of as the concealing factor. By the word prakṛti, the meaning of the BG text VII.14 is intended. According to Viśiṣṭādvaita, the word māyā conveys prakṛti or the wondrous power of God, and not an indeterminable entity. Cf. GSR, p. 52 and GS.V, pp. 19–20.
  2. The whole-hearted surrender is to be made to the Lord in order to get released from the clutches of this concealment. Thus, the import of the BG text “those who surrender to me alone transcend this māyā” (VII.14) is explained. Cf. GS.V., p. 20.
  3. The four types of devotees are ārta (one who has lost his position and wealth and who desires to regain them), [[P84]] arthārthī (one who aspires for wealth), jijñāsu (one who seeks to attain knowledge), and jñānī (one who longs to enjoy the Supreme bliss). See Bh.G.bha., p. 20. Their difference is due to their actions performed in earlier births. Cf. GS.V., p. 20.
  4. Since the devotee (jñānī) thinks of the Lord always and has a sole desire for His attainment, He is superior to others. GS.V., p. 20.

ŚLOKA XII

The distinction among the three classes of meditators, namely those who opt for material pleasure,51 those who seek self-realisation,52 and those who desire the service to the lotus feet of the Supreme Lord which is the Supreme bliss, and the knowledge and practice in regard to these devotees53 are explained in the eighth chapter.

NOTES:

  1. Material pleasure here means enjoyment surpassing the enjoyment of gods like Brahmā, Rudra and others. See GSR, p. 55 and GS.V, p. 20.

[[P85]]

  1. Self-realisation is the enjoyment of the individual soul devoid of insentient matter. See GS. Tamil Commentary of H.H. the 42nd Pontiff, p. 9.
  2. The true knowledge of the self and the practice related to the former. See GS.V., pp. 20–21 and GSR, p. 55.

ŚLOKA XIII

His (the Lord’s) own greatness,54 the superiority of Him even though He incarnates Himself among mortals,55 the superiority of the devotees,56 and bhakti-yoga are detailed57 in the ninth chapter.

NOTES:

  1. By the word svamāhātmyam occurring in the text, the meaning of the BG text (IX.4) “everything is pervaded by me” etc., becomes explained.
  2. “Not knowing My true nature who is the Lord of all, fools disregard Me in this mortal form.” BG. IX.11.

[[P86]]

  1. “Those who resort to the Lord through their various noble activities and thereby become released from transmigratory existence know my divine nature.” Cf. Bh.G.bha., p. 20. Hence, they are superior to others.
  2. In the ninth chapter, bhakti-yoga is treated as the important subject matter. Cf. GSR., p. 56.

ŚLOKA XIV

The knowledge that the Lord possesses innumerable auspicious qualities and that the entire world is under His control is elaborately explained in the tenth chapter58 in order that it may result in bhakti and grow in intensity.59

NOTES:

  1. In the ninth chapter, bhakti-yoga along with its aiding disciplines has been mentioned. Here in the tenth chapter, in order that such a bhakti may get accomplished, the innumerable auspicious qualities of the Lord, His sovereignty, and the fact that everything forms His body, are elaborated.

[[P87]]

  1. The divine qualities are mentioned again, so as to develop this bhakti in its intensity. With this idea, the word bhūyaḥ is used in the Gītā text X.1.

ŚLOKA XV

In the eleventh is stated the bestowing of the divine vision (to Arjuna) to comprehend the Supreme Lord,60 in His true cosmic form,61 and also loving devotion as the sole means of realising and attaining Him.62

NOTES:

  1. In the tenth chapter, it was stated that the Supreme Lord is the abode of all auspicious qualities, is the inner-self of all beings, and hence has everything as His body. Arjuna desires to have such a vision of the Supreme Lord, and the Lord complied with his request by providing the divine vision. Cf. Bh.G.bha., p. 392; also cf. GS.V., pp. 22–23.

[[P88]]

  1. The true cosmic form of the Lord is explained in Chapter XI, Ślokas 10–13.
  2. Realisation means the knowledge of the true nature of the Lord. The word vidiḥ means knowledge. The Lord is to be attained only by single-minded devotion, and this is meant by the word prāpti and is summarised in Gītā, XI.54.

ŚLOKA XVI

The superiority of bhakti,63 the means of accomplishing that, the non-capability of those who are unable to pursue such a meditation upon the self,64 the requirements for such a meditation,65 and the devotion suffused with love to the Supreme Lord66—these are discussed in the twelfth chapter.

NOTES:

  1. In contrast to meditation upon the individual soul, the superiority of meditation upon the Lord is stated in the twelfth chapter. It is because meditation upon the individual soul is difficult to practice [[P89]] and attaining the fruit is delayed. On the contrary, meditation upon the Supreme Lord is easy and it gives rise to the desired end quickly. See GS.V., p. 23; also cf. BG.XII-3 ff.
  1. If one is unable to focus the mind upon the Lord, then one should devote oneself to service, such as the devotional acts of constructing temples, lighting lamps, garlanding flowers, etc. Cf. GS.V, p. 24; also the Comm.Bh.G.Bhā., pp. 392–393.
  2. The requirements of karma-yoga are stated in Gītā text XII.13 ff.
  3. The meaning of the text (BG.XII.20), “those who follow this nectar-like righteous duty with full-fledged faith are exceedingly dear to Me,” is summarised here. See GS.V, p. 25.

ŚLOKA XVII

In the thirteenth chapter is explained the nature of the physical body,67 the means by which self-realisation can [[P90]] be attained,68 the nature of the soul that resides in the body,69 the cause of transmigration,70 and realising the true nature of the soul as being of the nature of knowledge.71

NOTES:

  1. The nature of the physical body is that it is constituted of the five elements. By this, the meaning of Gītā text XIII.5 and 6 becomes explained. Cf. GS.V, p. 26 and Bh.G.Bhā., p. 408.
  1. See Bh.G.Bhā. XIII.7–9.
  2. Cf. Bh.G.Bhā. XIII.12–17.
  3. The prakṛti or insentient matter which is subservient to the self is alone the causal matter: puruṣādhiṣṭhitā prakṛtireva hetuḥ (Bh.G.Bhā., p. 413).
  4. Realisation is the discrimination between the body and the soul and the knowledge that the individual soul is of the form of knowledge. This is possible by bhakti-yoga, jñāna-yoga and karma-yoga. Cf. GS.V., p. 26.

[[P91]]

ŚLOKA XVIII

In the fourteenth chapter are detailed the nature of the qualities that cause transmigratory existence,72 their agency,73 the means by which they are transcended,74 and the fact that He (the Lord) grants the three kinds of goals.75

NOTES:

  1. The fourteenth chapter of the Gītā is only an elaboration of the previous chapter. In the thirteenth, the nature of the individual soul, its association with the insentient entity, their true nature, etc., are said. In the ensuing chapter, the manner in which the qualities bind the individual soul and the means by which they are transcended are explained. GSR, p. 65.
    Sattva causes attachment to pleasure and to the knowledge related to those pleasures, rajas engages one in the activities of sense objects, tamas causes laziness, sleep, etc. Cf. GS.V, p. 27.
  1. Although the individual soul is the actual agent, the reason for ascribing agency to the qualities is to highlight the way to get released from them. Cf. GS.V, p. 27; GSR, p. 65. Also cf. BG.XIV.26.
  2. The means of their removal is mentioned thus: “One who meditates upon me with steadfast devotion, he, after transcending the qualities, becomes qualified to realise his own self” (BG.XIV.26).
  3. The three kinds of goals are the enjoyment of material pleasures, kaivalya (which is the enjoyment of one’s own self), and liberation. Cf. GS.V, p. 27; GSR, p. 65. Also cf. BG.XIV.27.

ŚLOKA XIX

Since the Supreme Person pervades everything and supports everything, and since He is the principal entity (śeṣi), He76 is different from the individual soul who is associated with the insentient matter, and from the individual soul who is77 released from that. This is explained in the fifteenth chapter.

NOTES:

  1. That the Lord pervades everything and supports everything is stated in the BG verse XV.17. Hence, He is different from all other entities.

[[P93]]

  1. The bound souls are termed kṣaras and the released souls are termed akṣaras. As the Supreme Lord is untouched by their imperfections, He is superior to them. Cf. Bh.G.bha., pp. 468–470.

ŚLOKA XX

In the sixteenth chapter is set forth the classification of78 gods, demons, etc., which is preceded by the statement79 that they are bound by the śāstras.80 The reason for making81 such a classification is that there may arise, from the said classification, a firm conviction (knowledge) in regard to the nature of reality and the means of attaining that, which can be had only from the śāstras.

NOTES:

  1. In the previous three chapters, that is, in the thirteenth, fourteenth and fifteenth, the nature of the individual soul devoid of prakṛti, the nature of the soul embodied in that prakṛti, the fact that association is due to attachment to the qualities, the means of release from that, the fact that both prakṛti and individual souls form the vibhūti of the Lord, and the true nature of the Lord were explained. Here, in order to impress that submission to the śāstras is essential for gaining faith in the teachings given earlier, the Lord begins His teachings on this topic. Cf. Bh.G.bha., p. 495.
  1. BG. XVI.16.
  2. ibid., XVI.4.
  3. The purpose of such a division as gods and demons is to make clear that the attainment of the knowledge of Reality and the knowledge of the means of that attainment here roots only in the Vedas. Cf. GS.V, p. 29 and GSR., p. 71.

[[P94]]

ŚLOKA XXI

All activities that are not enjoined by the śāstras82 are termed demonic (asura); the enjoined duties are distinctly threefold83 because the performance of them [[P95]] depends upon the three qualities, and the enjoined duties are performed as associated with three characteristics—these are dealt with in the seventeenth chapter.84

NOTES:

  1. Cf. aśāstravihitaṃ ghoraṃ tapyante, BG. XVII.5. Also see: śāstraṃ yasya vidhāyakatvena asti, and GS.V, p. 30; GSR, p. 73.
  1. Arjuna, on hearing that every being is bound by the śāstra, wants to clarify the position of persons who have faith but, laying aside the śāstra, perform sacrifices, etc. In this chapter, this doubt is cleared by making these classifications. Bh.G.bha., pp. 516–517. This is threefold as marked by sattva, rajas and tamas. Cf. tataḥ iyaṃ śraddhā sāttvikī rājasī tāmasī ca iti trividhā, Bh.G.bha. XVII.2, and śāstrīyeṣu yajñādiṣu guṇato viśeṣaṃ prapañcayati, Bh.G.bha. XVII.6, p. 520.

[[P96]]

  1. “Brahman is denoted by the threefold expression: praṇava, tat and sat. Associated with these, men of spiritual birth, the Vedas, and sacrifices were enjoined.” BG. XVII.23. Of these, the praṇava letter is uttered at the beginning of the recital of the Vedas; acts that are performed without aiming for results are designated by tat; and sat denotes auspicious acts such as austerities and gifts. BG. XVII.24–27.

ŚLOKA XXII

In the last chapter is explained the knowledge that the Supreme controller is the agent of all actions,85 that the quality sattva alone is to be adopted,86 that the performance of the enjoined duties according to one’s stage and class of life leads to the attainment of the Lord,87 and the kernel truth of the Gītāśāstra, namely bhakti-yoga.88

NOTES:

  1. For all actions that are performed, there are five [[P97]] causes. They are:
    a) the physical body, which is the modification of the five elements, is the substratum;
    b) the individual soul, who is the performer of those actions;
    c) the five motor organs along with the mind;
    d) the different functions of the vital air; and
    e) the divine will.
    The fifth cause, namely the divine will, which is none other than the Supreme Lord, is the main cause in accomplishing actions. He is the main cause because He is the inner self of all and controls everything, remaining within one’s self.
  1. An objection is raised in regard to the above view: if the agency lies only with the Supreme Lord, then injunctions enjoined in the śāstras would be devoid of purpose, because the individual souls alone are enjoined by them. This objection is removed by replying that the Supreme Lord grants the senses, body, etc., to the individual souls, who begin to act out of their own free will, having the Supreme Being for support. The Lord accords His permission and remains as a witness. This is similar to many persons moving a rock or a [[P98]] big tree; all of them are collectively and individually the agents. Similarly, the individual soul is enjoined by the śāstras. Vide: Bh.G.bha. XVIII.14–15, pp. 528–529. Cf. BG.XVIII.30; also cf. GS.V, p. 32 and GSR, p. 74.
  1. When a person is devoted to his duty in accordance with his class and stage of life as enjoined in the Gītāśāstra—that is, while performing those duties, worshipping the Supreme Lord—one should relinquish the agency and attachment to results.
