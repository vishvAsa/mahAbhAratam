## INTRODUCTION

The Vedas constitute the basic text for all the ancient religions that are in vogue in India. It is because of this reason that they are often referred to as Vaidika systems. The Smṛtis, Itihāsas, and Purāṇas are meant to clarify and determine the true import of the Vedas. Of these, the Smṛtis are termed as dharmaśāstras, and their main aim is to clarify and explain the ideas contained in the former portion of the Vedas, namely, karma-kāṇḍa. The Itihāsas and Purāṇas are for the purpose of clarifying the ideas contained in the latter portion of the Vedas, namely, jñāna-kāṇḍa. Among the Itihāsas and Purāṇas, the Itihāsas are more valid than the Purāṇas. It is because, while the Purāṇas are classified as Sāttvika, Rājasa, and Tāmasa, the latter ones are less authoritative. There is no such classification in regard to the Itihāsas.

The Itihāsas are well known as the Rāmāyaṇa and the Mahābhārata. Though both of them are of equal importance and validity, yet the Mahābhārata is held in high esteem [[P2]] and is considered as the fifth Veda (*bhārataḥ pañcamo vedaḥ*). The name given to this text, initially, was `jaya`[^2_1] by the author-sage Vyāsa. But later it came to be known as Mahābhārata because of its greatness and because of the subject matter discussed in depth. This is considered to be the highest authority due to the greatness of the content, greatness of the text, and the greatness of the author (*viṣaya-gaurava*, *prabandha-gaurava*, and *vaktṛ-gaurava*).

[^2_1]: The opening śloka of the Mahābhārata is:  
*nārāyaṇaṃ namaskṛtya naraṃ caiva narottamama / devīṃ sarasvatīṃ vyāsaṃ tato jayamudīrayet*

The Bhagavad Gītā occurs in the Bhīṣmaparva of the Mahābhārata and constitutes eighteen chapters, from the 25th to the 42nd. This is considered to be the core part of the Mahābhārata, and the greatness of the Bhagavad Gītā can be understood from the fact that it is referred to by the ancient preceptors as Śrīmad Bhagavad Gītā, and from the often-quoted statement: [[P3]]

> *vedoṣu pauruṣaṃ sūktaṃ  
> dharmāśāstreṣu mānavam /  
> bhārate bhagavadgītā purāṇeṣu ca vaiṣṇavam //*

The Bhagavad Gītā, along with the Upaniṣads and the Brahma-sūtras, forms the prasthānatraya and represents the primary texts of the Vedānta systems of thought. The religious preceptors offered explanations and clarifications to the above texts in their commentaries and thereby sought to establish their religious and metaphysical concepts.

The commentaries on the Bhagavad Gītā in particular are more in number than the other two, namely, the Upaniṣads and the Brahma-sūtras. The author of the Brahma-sūtras recognises the authority of the Bhagavad Gītā in the aphorisms *smṛteśca*[^3_1] and *smaranti ca*.[^3_2] Further, two reasons—namely, the greatness of the person who uttered the text and the context in which it was uttered—enhance the greatness and importance of the text.

[^3_1]: *Brahma-sūtra* I.ii.6.
[^3_2]: *ibid.*, IV.i.10.

[[P4]] Lord Kṛṣṇa, in order to dispel the ignorance of Arjuna—who became desperate and plunged into a state of despondency on seeing his close relatives in the battlefield—uttered the Bhagavad Gītā as requested by Arjuna when he made a wholehearted surrender to the Lord thus:

> *kārpaṇyadoṣopahatasvabhāvaḥ*  
> *pṛcchāmi tvāṃ dharmasammūḍhacetāḥ /‌*  
> *yacchreyaḥ syān niścitaṃ brūhi tanme*  
> *śiṣyaste'haṃ śādhi māṃ tvāṃ prapannam*  
> (BG. II.7)

As this text was uttered to dispel ignorance, it is implied that it contains the highest truth; hence, whatever is stated in the Bhagavad Gītā is to be taken as the ultimate truth. Thus, the Bhagavad Gītā is meant for the upliftment of the entire society, emerging from the mouth of the Supreme Lord under the pretext of advising Arjuna. Thus, the Bhagavad Gītā [[P5]] is the summum bonum of the Upanishadic teachings.[^5_1]

[^5_1]: Cf. p. 17 of this dissertation.

Though there are several commentaries available upon the Bhagavad Gītā, the commentaries of Śaṅkara, Madhva, and Rāmānuja are noteworthy. They based their interpretations of the Bhagavad Gītā upon their respective doctrines, namely, Advaita, Dvaita, and Viśiṣṭādvaita.

In the history of the Viśiṣṭādvaita tradition, the first work on the Bhagavad Gītā is the Gītārthasaṅgraha of Yāmunācārya. In the long line of the Śrīvaiṣṇava preceptors, Nāthamuni (alias Raṅganāthamuni) was the first ācārya who promulgated the teachings of the Upaniṣads and the Bhagavad Gītā. He was succeeded by his grandson Yāmunācārya, who is well known as Āḷavandār in Tamil. He is the author of six works, namely, the Āgamaprāmāṇya, the Siddhitraya, the Gītārthasaṅgraha, the Puruṣanirṇaya, the Catuḥślokī, and the Stotraratna. As the very name suggests, [[P6]] in the Gītārthasaṅgraha, the author gives the essence of the Bhagavad Gītā in a nutshell. It consists of 32 ślokas set in anuṣṭubh metre. In the first verse, the author states that Lord Nārāyaṇa is primarily conveyed in the Gītāśāstra. Then, in the next three verses, the import of the three ṣaṭkas (comprising six chapters each) is presented. In the following eighteen ślokas, the author summarises the teachings contained in the eighteen chapters of the Gītāśāstra. The last ten verses are devoted to the true nature of karma-yoga, jñāna-yoga, and bhakti-yoga for the purpose of easy comprehension.

Based upon the Gītārthasaṅgraha, Rāmānuja, who succeeded Yāmuna, wrote the commentary *Bhagavadgītā-bhāṣya*. This commentary of Rāmānuja, as observed in a close study of the introductory portion of each chapter, strictly adheres to the Gītārthasaṅgraha and can be described as a commentary on the Gītārthasaṅgraha of his preceptor. Rāmānuja's indebtedness is exhibited in the introductory verse of his *Bhagavadgītā-bhāṣya*: [[P7]]

> *yatpadāmboruha-dhyāna-vidhvastāśeṣa-kalmaṣaḥ /  
> vastutam upāyato'ham yāmuneyaṃ namāmi tam //*

Throughout the commentary, Rāmānuja maintains an elegant and attractive style and brings out the essence of the Bhagavad Gītā in a relishable and delightful manner.

Vekaṭanātha, well known as Vedānta Deśika, was the most important writer of the post-Rāmānuja period. He was the author of more than a hundred works. He wrote two works on the Bhagavad Gītā, namely, the *Gītārthasaṅgraharakṣā* and the *Tātparyacandrikā*. The former is a commentary on the work of Yāmunācārya and was written to defend the work of that ācārya from the criticisms of rival schools. The style of the *Gītārthasaṅgraharakṣā* reveals the scholarship and mastery of Vedānta Deśika over the subject. The *Tātparyacandrikā* is a commentary on the *Bhagavadgītā-bhāṣya* of Rāmānuja. The *Tātparyacandrikā* is very exhaustive and informative, [[P8]] and it exhibits Vedānta Deśika's devotion to Rāmānuja and his teachings.

### The Commentary Gītārthasaṅgraharakṣāvibhāga

The present commentary on the Gītārthasaṅgraha is the first and perhaps the only one of this nature in the post-Vedānta Deśika period. It has already been stated that the Gītārthasaṅgraha of Yāmunācārya was the first text in the Viśiṣṭādvaita tradition to summarise the Bhagavad Gītā in a nutshell. Vedānta Deśika chose to comment on the minor works of Yāmunācārya, namely, the Catuḥślokī, the Stotraratna, and the Gītārthasaṅgraha. The commentary on the Gītārthasaṅgraha is known as *Rakṣā*, which is one of the five *Rakṣās* composed by Vedānta Deśika, the other four being *Nikṣeparakṣā*, *Pañcarātrarakṣā*, *Rahasyarakṣā*, and *Saccaritrarakṣā*. The popularity of Vedānta Deśika led subsequent writers either to write commentaries or abridgements of the original works of Rāmānuja and Vedānta Deśika. The present commentary *Vibhāga* is [[P9]] on an original work of the pre-Rāmānuja period, which is perhaps the first of its kind, making it highly significant. Following this, writers in the succeeding centuries started writing commentaries on the minor works of earlier ācāryas like Śrīvatsāṅkamiśra, Parāśara Bhaṭṭa, etc.

### The Commentator

The author of this commentary is one Nārāyaṇamuni, and it appears from the name that he belonged to the ascetic order. This Nārāyaṇamuni belonged to the order of sannyāsins of the Śrī Ahobila Math tradition, which is evident from the maṅgala ślokas and from the colophon of the text. However, as there were six or seven pontiffs[^9_1] with the name Nārāyaṇamuni in the Ahobila Math tradition, who exactly is the author of this commentary cannot be definitively known.

[^9_1]: See Prof. V.S. Seshadri's thesis, p. 260.

The Ahobila Math was founded in 1398 A.D. by Śrī Ādivaṇ Śaṭhakopa Yatindra Mahādeśikan, who was the first [[P10]] Pontiff of that monastery.[^10_1] He was followed by Śrī Nārāyaṇamuni. This Nārāyaṇamuni, according to tradition, is the author of sixty works and is termed as *ṣaṣṭiprabandha-nirmātā*.[^10_2] However, some are of the view that *ṣaṣṭiprabandha* is only the name of a work, and hence he is referred to as the author of that work.[^10_3] Important among his works are the *Īśāvāsyopaniṣad-bhāṣya*, *Ārādhanasaṅgrahakārikā*, *Nyāsaviṃśativyākhyā*, *Aṣṭaślokīvyākhyā*, *sandhyāvandanabhāṣya*, etc. The Guruparamparā text mentions[^10_4] that he also composed *Puruṣakāramīmāṃsā* dealing with the concept of Śrī/Lakṣmī as upāya and upeya. However, a recent thesis submitted by Mrs. G. Sakuntala to the University of Madras in 1993, in which the text of *Puruṣakāramīmāṃsā* is appended, shows that this work was composed by Nārāyaṇamuni who was the fourteenth Pontiff of Ahobila Math. The reason, according to the author of the thesis, is that the taniyan (dedicatory) verse [[P11]] in praise of the 14th Pontiff mentioned in the text[^11_1] differs from that of the 2nd Pontiff,[^11_2] who was also known as Nārāyaṇamuni. They can be differentiated from each other by the fact that the 2nd Pontiff was known as Śrīvatsāṅka Śrīman Nārāyaṇamuni, while the 14th Pontiff was simply known as Nārāyaṇamuni.

[^10_1]: See Prof. V.S. Seshadri's thesis, p. 260.
[^10_2]: See *History of Visistadvaita Literature*, p. 55.
[^10_3]: See Mrs. G. Sakuntala's thesis, pp. 2–4.
[^10_4]: See *Guruparampara of Ahobila Math*, p. 20.

[^11_1]: *śrīvīrarāghavamuneḥ varivasyāikajīvanam / samāśrayāmahi śrīmannārāyaṇamunīśvaram* (See *Acaryavaibhava*, p. 287)
[^11_2]: *śrīśaṭhāriyatīśānapadapaṅkajasatpadam / śrīmannārāyaṇamuniṃ śraye śrībhāṣyadeśikam* (ibid., p. 269)

It is interesting to note that the 2nd Pontiff is ascribed with the authorship of the present commentary by one of the writers.[^11_3] The reason for this may be the identity in the names of these Pontiffs. However, the colophon of the commentary contains the taniyan verse of the 14th Pontiff, and this points to him as the author of the commentary *Gītārthasaṅgrahavibhāga*. There is a remote possibility that the work was composed by the 2nd Pontiff himself, and that a disciple of [[P12]] the 14th Pontiff copied the text, thereby paying obeisance to his own preceptor. However, this is unlikely to be the case, as it was a customary practice in the tradition for a writer to pay obeisance to his preceptor by mentioning the taniyan or composing another maṅgala śloka in his honour.

[^11_3]: See *History of Visistadvaita Literature*, p. 54.

Another reason indicating that the commentary *Gītārthasaṅgrahavibhāga* was composed by the 14th Pontiff is: in the beginning of the commentary on the *Aṣṭaślokī* (written by the 2nd Pontiff), the author's taniyan is clearly mentioned, and towards the end, the author refers to himself[^12_1] as Śrīvatsāṅkamuni. Similarly, the concluding verse of the *sandhyāvandanabhāṣya* of the 2nd Pontiff contains obeisance to his preceptor Śrī Ādivaṇ Śaṭhakopa and also mentions that he was the author of sixty (*ṣaṣṭi*)[^12_2] prabandhas. But the *Gītārthasaṅgraharakṣāvibhāga* [[P13]] contains no such mentions anywhere in the text.

[^12_1]: See *Aṣṭaślokī* commentary beginning and concluding verses.
[^12_2]: Cf. *Sandhyavandanabhaṣya*, p. 53.

Apart from the above works, there are several other works attributed to Nārāyaṇamuni noticed by scholars. They include *Jijñāsāsūtrabhāṣyabhāvaprakāśikā*, *Rahasyatrayajīvātu*, *Stotraratnabhāṣya*, *Nyāsadoṣavyākhyā*, *Pūrvapakṣamīmāṃsā*, *Siddhāntamīmāṃsā*, *Puruṣārthasudhānidhi*, and *Gītārthasaṅgrahasāra*. Of these, except for the *Gītārthasaṅgrahasāra*[^13_1] and barring the *Stotraratnabhāṣya*, all the other works seem to be the creations of the 2nd Pontiff, Śrīvatsāṅka Śrīman Nārāyaṇamuni. The *Gītārthasaṅgrahasāra*[^13_2] is definitely by the same author as the *Gītārthasaṅgraharakṣāvibhāga*, as the author mentions the former in the latter work. The exact authorship of the *Stotraratnabhāṣya* has not yet been proved.

[^13_1]: See *History of Visistadvaita Literature*, pp. 54–55.
[^13_2]: See p. [Page number blank in MS] of this dissertation.

### Style of the Commentary

The style of the present text is very simple and lucid. The Gītārthasaṅgraha of Yāmunācārya condenses [[P14]] all the points contained in the Bhagavad Gītā, and as the author himself states in one of the maṅgala ślokas, the meanings of the verses are to be comprehended with great effort. Vedānta Deśika made it easier to know the meaning of the verses. But it was Nārāyaṇamuni who precisely pointed out the division of the verses in the Bhagavad Gītā that were referred to by the indicatory words of Yāmunācārya in his ślokas and chapters. Hence, the commentary can rightly be said to be an analysis of the contents of the Gītārthasaṅgraha, and was appropriately named *Vibhāga*. Another point to be noted about the commentary is that it not only comments on the original text but also refers to the commentary *Rakṣā* at a few places. Though there may be some unclear ideas or lacunae, as in the case of the opening śloka (which cannot otherwise be checked since there is only one manuscript available), yet the meaning can be comprehended easily even upon a first reading. Hence, the text may be said to be a prolegomena [[P15]] to the study of the Bhagavad Gītā from the viewpoint of Viśiṣṭādvaita.

## मूलम्[[P16]]

• श्रीश्रीवत्साङ्कनारायणमुनिना अनुगृहीता

भगवद्गीतार्थसंग्रहविभागः

[^16_1]श्रीमद्धंसिहयग्रीवहंसजुष्टेऽतिपाण्डरे ।  
पुण्डरीके मम मनः चञ्चरीकः प्रवर्तताम्॥

[^65_1]: त्रयोदशचतुर्दशपञ्चदशषोडशाध्यायार्थाः।
[^65_2]: सप्तदशाष्टादशाध्यायार्थाः।

### ŚLOKA I

It is clearly established in the Śāstra, namely Bhagavadgītā that Lord Nārāyaṇa is the Supreme Brahman,[^68_1] who is to be attained by bhakti alone.[^68_2] And, that bhakti is to be accomplished by the performance of one's own prescribed rites,[^68_3] by the knowledge of the true nature of the self[^68_4] and by non-attachment to worldly objects.[^68_5]

#### NOTES:

1. According to Viśiṣṭādvaita, Lord Nārāyaṇa along with his consort Lakṣmī is the Supreme Reality. The Bhagavadgītā, which is an epitome of the Upaniṣads, mentions Him as the Supreme Reality. See also *GS.V.*, p. 2:
   > *tattvaṃ jijñāsamānānāṃ hetubhiḥ sarvatomukhaiḥ /‌*  
   > *tattvam eko mahāyogī harirnārāyaṇātparaḥ //‌*

   See p. 2 of the present dissertation: "ataḥ sakalopaniṣat-sāra-saṅkalanātmikāsu bhagavadgītāsvapi para-tattva-bhūta-para-brahmaiva prādhānyena pratipādyam."

[^68_1]: According to Viśiṣṭādvaita, Lord Nārāyaṇa along with his consort Lakṣmī is the Supreme Reality. The Bhagavadgītā, which is an epitome of the Upaniṣads, mentions Him as the Supreme Reality. See also *GS.V.*, p. 2: *tattvaṃ jijñāsamānānāṃ hetubhiḥ sarvatomukhaiḥ / tattvam eko mahāyogī harirnārāyaṇātparaḥ //* See p. 2 of the present dissertation: *ataḥ sakalopaniṣadsārasaṅkalanātmikāsu bhagavadgītāsvapi paratattvabhūtaparabrahmaiva prādhānyena pratipādyam.*

[[P69]]

2. *mahanīyaviṣaye prītiḥ bhaktiḥ*. Bhakti is the sole means of attaining the Supreme, is stated thus: "bhaktaikalabhye puruṣe purāṇe..." See *GS.V*, p. 3.
3. *Svadharmaḥ*. The word is interpreted as *sve dharmaḥ* (wherein the usage is in the locative case). It is also interpreted as *svasya dharmaḥ* (wherein the usage is with the genitive case-ending). By both interpretations, one's own enjoined duties in accordance with one's class and stage of life is intended. According to the commentator, the word *sva* at the beginning also conveys the sense of benediction. *GS.V*, p. 3. Cf. *ubhayathāpi svavarṇāśramadharma ityarthaḥ*, *GS.V*, p. 3.
4. The knowledge of the self means the knowledge of one's own self whose essential nature consists in being subordinate to the Supreme Lord: *jñānam atra pāraśeṣataikarasa-svātma-viṣayaṃ jñānam*, *GS.V*, p. 3.
5. "Non-attachment" (Vairāgya) means a strong dislike [[P70]] towards anything other than the Supreme Lord. Cf. *paramātmani yo rakto virakto'paramātmani*, *GS.V*, p. 3; also cf. GSR, p. 4.

### ŚLOKA II

   Jñānayoga is stated as: *jñānayogo nāma uktakarmayogena labdhādhikārasya karmayogatvaveṣaṃ parityajya nityanaimittikadharmānuṣṭhānam*, *GS.V*, p. 6.

### ŚLOKA III

In the next six chapters bhakti-yoga is expounded[^71_1] in detail[^71_2] which is accomplished by the performance of the jñāna-yoga and karma-yoga for the purpose of the enjoyment of the Supreme Reality, namely, Bhagavan[^71_3] in His true nature.[^71_4]

### ŚLOKA IV

Primordial matter,[^72_1] the nature of the individual souls,[^72_2] the evolutes of prakṛti such as mahat etc.,[^72_3] the nature of the Lord who is the controller of all these entities,[^72_4] the distinction between them,[^72_5] karma-yoga, jñāna-yoga, bhakti-yoga [[P73]] and such other issues which subserve the previous two parts are discussed in the last six chapters.

### ŚLOKA XXII

   d) the different functions of the vital air; and  
   e) the divine will.

> *tatra śrīrvijayo bhūtiḥ*  
> *dhruvā nītirmatirmama //‌*

"Wherever is Krishna, Yoga's Lord, wherever is Pārtha, the archer, assured are there prosperity, Victory and happiness. So I think."  
**BG: 18:78**
