## Mercury Venus
An uncommon astronomical simile using the conjunction of Mercury and Venus:

> tato droNash cha pArthash cha  
> sameyAtAM mahAmR^idhe |
yathA budhash cha shukrash  
> cha mahArAja nabhastale || Mbh 6.097.057

> Then, O great king [dhR^itarAShTra], droNa and pArtha came together in the great battle, just as the [conjunction of] Mercury and Venus, the celestial vault.

Often, the bright planets Venus and Jupiter are paired with each other in Sanskrit similes: their pairing also denotes the clash of the teachers, the asura-aligned shukra and the deva-aligned bR^ihaspati. 

A more uncommon simile is the conjunction of Mercury and Venus. However, this rather than the former is encountered in the mahAbharata in a military context.  Their relatively greater speed of orbit might lend value to this simile involving the clash of arjuna and droNa. As inner planets, these two cross paths more frequently (once in 6-8 months) than Venus with Jupiter (~once in 13 months); however, most of the former conjunctions are lost in the glare of the sun. Hence, a Mercury-Venus conjunction with "good" visibility is a rarer proposition and not commonly seen by moderns.

## Mercury Mars
The mahAbhArata likes to invoke multiple military similes with conjunctions of Mercury with different planets. Describing the battle between the bAhlika on the kuru side and chedi-rAja dR^iShTaketu on the pANDava side, saMjaya says:

> tau tatra samare kruddhau  
> nardantau cha muhur muhuH |
samIyatuH susaMkruddhAv  
> a~NgAraka-budhAv iva || Mbh 6.43.38

> Those two, enraged there in the battle, roaring again and again, came together in great fury, like the [conjunction] of Mars and Mercury.

## Mercury Saturn
> samAgatau tau tu raNe mahAmAtrau vyarochatAm |
yathA divi mahAghorau rAjan budha-shanaishcharau || Mbh 6.100.20

> Those two of great stature, having met in battle, shone resplendently, just as the two most formidable \[planets], Mercury and Saturn, do in the heavens, O King.

Here we have the conjunctions of Mercury with Mars and Saturn, respectively. While these might happen once or more in a year in principle, visible conjunctions of these planets are quite rare. Hence, it is notable that the national epic chooses these conjunctions for similes. 

While in the pre-modern skies over open spaces, these conjunctions might have been quite striking just after sunset or before sunrise, for most modern city dwellers, they are a difficult proposition. In any case, given the relative rarity of good visible versions of these conjunctions, we wonder if there was a special reason why they were chosen by the epic authors.
